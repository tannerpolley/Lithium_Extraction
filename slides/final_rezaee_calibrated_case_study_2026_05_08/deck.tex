\begin{frame}[plain]
  \titlepage
  \note{
    I am going to walk through a produced-water lithium case study as an
    integration package, not as a broad market overview. The chain is deliberately
    narrow: start with a Smackover feed, define what pretreatment removes, map
    the Rezaee TBAC/DA DES plus TOPO Li/Na response into compact transfer terms,
    route those terms through an ALAMO surrogate, and then carry them into
    PrOMMiS/IDAES staged extraction and screening TEA. The reason to keep the
    chain this explicit is that each layer has a different job. Feed chemistry
    defines the severity. The solvent response defines selectivity. The process
    model defines staged recovery. The economics tell us which improvements are
    worth chasing first.
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[standout]
A pretreated Smackover Li/Na stream connects realistic produced-water chemistry
to staged extraction and screening-level economics.
\note{
  The thesis is that the useful technical unit is not a raw brine and not a
  stand-alone extractant number. The useful unit is a pretreated Li/Na stream
  whose transfer behavior can be propagated through stages and costs. That is
  why the case study starts from produced-water chemistry, but it does not try
  to put every raw-brine component into the contactor. The divalent and oil-side
  burdens are handled at the boundary. What moves forward is the Li/Na transfer
  problem: how much lithium moves, how much sodium follows, and how those two
  numbers change the process result.
}
\end{frame}

\section{Case Basis}
\sectiondivider{Case Basis}{
  I am starting with the feed because that is the part of the story where
  produced-water realism matters most. Lithium extraction can look artificially
  clean if the feed basis is vague. Here, the feed basis is tied to a southern
  Arkansas Smackover row, and the rest of the section explains why that row is a
  useful main case rather than just another sensitivity point.
}

  }

\sectiondivider{Solvent And Transfer Surface}{

  this section is simple: once the pretreated Li/Na stream reaches the solvent,

  process model?
}

\begin{frame}{Paper-Response Variables Feed The Process Model}
  \begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
      \[
        D_i = \frac{E_i}{100-E_i}\frac{1}{O/A}
      \]
      \[
        \SLiNa = \frac{\DLi}{\DNa}
      \]
      \begin{exampleblock}{Rezaee anchors, $O/A=1$}
        Optimized 10 wt\% TOPO:
        $E_{\mathrm{Li}}=48.57\%$, $\SLiNa=4.41$,
        so $E_{\mathrm{Na}}=11.01\%$.

        Model-brine row:
        $E_{\mathrm{Li}}=51.63\%$ and $E_{\mathrm{Na}}=9.97\%$.
      \end{exampleblock}
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.49\textwidth}
      \transferpath{1.0}
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
  \note{
    This is where extraction percentages become process variables. For each
    species, the distribution coefficient is the extracted fraction divided by
    the raffinate fraction, corrected by the organic-to-aqueous ratio. Once
    $D_{\mathrm{Li}}$ and $D_{\mathrm{Na}}$ are defined, selectivity is just the
    ratio of those two coefficients. That conversion is important because staged
    extraction models do not need a paper table copied verbatim; they need
    transfer terms that can be applied stage by stage. The Rezaee anchor at
    $O/A=1$ gives 48.57 percent lithium extraction and 11.01 percent sodium
    extraction for the optimized TOPO case, which is enough to define a
    process-ready Li/Na transfer pair.
  }
\end{frame}

\section{Thermodynamic Extension}
\sectiondivider{Thermodynamic Extension}{
  The final section shows where ePC-SAFT enters the case study. The downstream
  process structure is already shaped to receive activity and fugacity based
  transfer rows without rebuilding the entire presentation.
}

\begin{frame}{ePC-SAFT Supplies Activity And Fugacity Terms}
  \tiny
  \begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
    \begin{column}{0.58\textwidth}
      \[
      \begin{aligned}
      \tilde{a}^{\mathrm{res}} &=
      \tilde{a}^{\mathrm{hc}}+\tilde{a}^{\mathrm{disp}}
      +\tilde{a}^{\mathrm{assoc}}+\tilde{a}^{\mathrm{DH}}
      +\tilde{a}^{\mathrm{Born}}\\
      \tilde{a}^{\mathrm{DH}} &=
      -\frac{\kappa e^{2}}{12\pi\varepsilon_{0}\varepsilon_{r}k_{B}T}
      \sum_i x_i z_i^2\chi_i\\
      \kappa &=
      \sqrt{\frac{\rho e^{2}}{k_{B}T\varepsilon_{0}\varepsilon_{r}}
      \sum_j x_j z_j^2}\\
      \chi_i &=
      \frac{3}{(\kappa d_i)^3}
      \left[\frac32+\ln(1+\kappa d_i)-2(1+\kappa d_i)
      +\frac12(1+\kappa d_i)^2\right]\\
      \tilde{a}^{\mathrm{Born}} &=
      -\frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 k_B T}
      \sum_i x_i z_i^2\sum_{\delta\in\mathcal{D}}
      \left(1-\frac{1}{\varepsilon_{r,m_\delta}}\right)
      D_{i,\delta}^{(m_\delta)}
      \end{aligned}
      \]
      \[
      \begin{aligned}
      D_{i,\mathrm{Born}}^{(\mathrm{bulk})}&=\frac{1}{d_i^{\mathrm{Born}}}\\
      D_{i,\mathrm{SSM}}^{(\mathrm{bulk})}&=
      \frac{1}{d_i^{\mathrm{Born}}+\Delta d_i}-\frac{1}{d_i^{\mathrm{Born}}}\\
      D_{i,\mathrm{DS}}^{(\mathrm{ion})}&=
      \frac{1}{d_i^{\mathrm{Born}}}-\frac{1}{d_i^{\mathrm{Born}}+\Delta d_i}
      \end{aligned}
      \]
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.39\textwidth}
      \begin{tikzpicture}[
        box/.style={rounded corners=1.5pt,draw=netlteal!62,fill=netlmist,
          line width=0.45pt,align=center,inner sep=3.2pt,font=\scriptsize,

        \node[box,below=of helm] (deriv) {$\mu_i^{\mathrm{res}}/RT$ and $Z$\\from composition and density derivatives};
        \node[box,below=of deriv] (terms) {$\ln \phi_i$ and $\ln \gamma_i$\\fugacity and activity terms};

      \[
        \tilde{\mu}_k^{\mathrm{res}} =
        \tilde{a}^{\mathrm{res}}+
        Z-1+
        \left(\frac{\partial\tilde{a}^{\mathrm{res}}}{\partial x_k}\right)_{T,v,x_i}
      \]
      \[
        \ln \varphi_k=\tilde{\mu}_k^{\mathrm{res}}-\ln Z,\qquad
        a_i=\gamma_i x_i,\qquad
        Q=\prod_i a_i^{\nu_i}
      \]
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
  \vspace{0.02cm}
  {\tiny\color{netlgray}Shown terms are the Debye--Huckel finite-ion-size and modular Born inverse-diameter forms used for Li/Na activity and fugacity terms.}
  \note{
    This slide is the thermodynamic map in one place. ePC-SAFT starts from the
    residual Helmholtz energy and builds it from five physical contribution
    families: hard-chain packing and connectivity, dispersion attractions,
    association, Debye-Huckel electrolyte screening, and Born solvation or
    dielectric work. The Debye-Huckel term uses the screening parameter
    $\kappa$ and finite-size correction $\chi_i$ from the documented equation
    set. The Born term is the modular form: it sums active Born, solvation
    shell, and dielectric-saturation inverse-diameter terms through
    $\mathcal{D}$. Once these terms are assembled, composition and density
    derivatives give residual chemical potentials and the compressibility
    factor. Those become fugacity coefficients and activity terms. For
    extraction, the important output is the activity basis for the reaction
    quotient and for the Li and Na transfer rows that the staged process model
    consumes.
  }
\end{frame}

    requirements have to come first.
